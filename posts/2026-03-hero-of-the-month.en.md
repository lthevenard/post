# It’s all about the evidence

![Illustration: The commode of broken dreams](./assets/img/evidence_based_decision-en.png)

<br>

Naturally, regulatory policy is always grounded in solid empirical evidence. Regulatory impact analyses are conducted with impeccable methodological rigor, carefully weighing costs and benefits through objective models, neutral assumptions, and transparent data. Political considerations, lobbying pressures, institutional incentives, and strategic timing play no meaningful role in shaping the outcome.

Decisions simply follow where the evidence leads.

Occasionally, the evidence may require a bit of *clarification*. Some assumptions might be adjusted, certain parameters refined, and a few inconvenient uncertainties set aside. But this is merely part of the scientific process: ensuring that the analysis fully captures what the evidence was clearly suggesting all along.

After all, good regulation is evidence-based — and it is surely just a coincidence that the most appropriate interpretation of the evidence so rarely diverges from the political and institutional interests at stake, or from the decision that, quite independently, was already about to be made.
